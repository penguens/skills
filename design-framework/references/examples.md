# design review examples

each example maps to one or more penguen design principles. notice how the "good" version balances humanity, clarity, action, and delight.

## empty state

### bad

a blank screen that says "no items yet" with no further guidance. the user is left wondering what to do.

### good

a warm illustration, a single line explaining what will appear here, and a clear primary button: "create your first project."

**principles at work:** humanity (guided, not judged), action (clear next step), delight (thoughtful moment).

## error recovery

### bad

a red banner saying "error 500: internal server error." no context, no recovery path.

### good

a calm message: "we could not save your changes. your work is still here — tap retry or copy your text before closing." followed by retry and copy buttons.

**principles at work:** clarity (explains what happened), humanity (acknowledges the user's worry), action (retry + copy as safety net).

## busy dashboard

### bad

every metric, chart, and activity feed crammed into one view. equal visual weight on everything. the user has to hunt for what matters.

### good

one primary metric at the top with clear context. secondary metrics grouped below. each section has a concise label. hover reveals detail without cluttering the default view.

**principles at work:** clarity (hierarchy, progressive disclosure), action (each section suggests a next step).

## onboarding flow

### bad

a five-step wizard with walls of text explaining features. the user clicks "next" without reading. no sense of progress.

### good

three focused steps. each step does one thing: set up, personalize, start. concise copy explains why each step matters. a progress indicator shows position. the final step has a clear, exciting call to action.

**principles at work:** action (short, purposeful flow), clarity (one thing per step), delight (momentum and celebration on finish).

## notification

### bad

"you have 47 unread notifications" — vague, overwhelming, no clear action.

### good

"3 things need your attention this morning" — warm, specific, and grouped by priority. each item links to the relevant screen.

**principles at work:** humanity (respects attention), clarity (specific count, not vague), action (direct links).
